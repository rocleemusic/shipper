# Hook Selection Rubric

Gate 3 picks one lead hook. This is the decision order and what each sounds like.

## Decision order
1. **Does the build visibly do what a generic AI won't, on the same input?** → Duel demo. Strongest when it exists; it proves the point instead of claiming it.
2. **Is the builder credibly the user?** → Founder story. "Built by a [role] who [lived problem]."
3. **Is there a sharp named refusal?** → Anti-positioning line. "Most [category] are [weakness]. This one [refuses that]."

Pick the first that fits. One hook leads; the others can support in the body.

## What each sounds like
- **Duel:** "Same company, two AIs. One writes a confident paragraph. The other refuses to — and does the work."
- **Founder story:** "Built by a working [role] — [N] years on the frontline, productionized into a tool."
- **Anti-positioning:** "Most AI coaches are knowledge bases in a trench coat. This one isn't."

## Weak-hook rules (reject on any of these)
Gate 3 self-evaluates every hook — detected or proposed — against this list before leading with it. Trip a rule → reject, name which, re-propose. This list is **updateable**: when a weak hook slips through, add the rule that would have caught it.

1. **Category-swappable.** If swapping in a competitor's name leaves the line true, it isn't a hook. → Cut until it only describes this build.
2. **Claims instead of shows.** "Powerful," "intelligent," "smart" — adjectives a stranger can't verify. → Name the behavior they'd see, not the quality you assert.
3. **Buries the payoff.** Leads with architecture or methodology ("a four-gate decision system") instead of what it refuses or decides. → Lead with what the user feels; the method supports in the body.
4. **Founder story on a persona.** A "built by a [role]" hook where the builder isn't credibly that role. → Don't fake lived ground (Gate 4 flags it anyway). Switch to duel or anti-positioning.
5. **Can't be shown in one screen.** If the hook needs a paragraph to land, it won't survive a duel column or a 60-second short. → Compress to one line, or pick a hook that demonstrates.

When you reject, name the rule and the fix — don't just say "weak."
